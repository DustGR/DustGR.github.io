Conclusions

It is apparent that temperature is strongly correlated with lattitude. There appears to be weaker tendency for humidity to be lower in the ranges of -20° and 40°. It is more difficult to draw conclusions about wind speed's connection to lattitude, and there does not appear to be any trend between lattitude and clouds.


Role of Geography

The tendency of humidity to be high in equatorial and polar regions and lower in certain bands lattitude is likely due to the distribution of continents on the globe. Water is necessary for humidity, meaning coastal regions and small land masses are more prone to high humidity. In contrast, larger land masses can include more inland area, which may have less rainfall and humidity. Clouds are visible collections of water in the atmosphere, but surprisingly did not follow the same trends as humidity (if they followed any trend at all). Similarly, wind can be the result of a storm or other event, but may occur more frequently in certain valleys or coastal areas due to temperature differences. The behavior and distribution of wind, clouds, and humidity are more likely explained by regional geography than by distance from the equator.


Notes and post-mortem

Please note that there are very few places measuring conditions south of -60° lattitude, and none were randomly selected by this dataset. This results in the graphs beginning at -60° lattitude at the X origin point, and ending at 80° lattitude. In other words, the extreme left edge of the graphs do not represent the same distance from the equator as the extreme right side of the graph.
This project was intended to show proficiency in HTML and CSS to present data, rather than to thoroughly explore weather and meteorology. In a more extended project, it would behoove me to include maps and citations to discuss geography.
