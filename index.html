Analysis of Weather by Lattitude

Lattitude is an angular measurement of location relative to the Earth's equator. A location at 0° is on the Earth's equator, while one at 90° (or 90° North) is at the north pole, and one at -90° (or 90° south) is at the south pole. Because the Earth has a slight tilt compared to the sun, which varies at different times of year, areas closer to the equator receive more consistent exposure to sunlight over the course of a year. Conversely, polar areas sometimes experience extremely short days in winter, or days with constant low sunlight in summer (known as Midnight Sun or White Nights)

It follows that with different amounts of sunlight, different lattitudes would have different atmospheric conditions. The most obvious difference is temperature. Are there other differences? In an earlier assignment, U of A Data Bootcamp students used public weather data from Open Weather Map API to compare weather observations at different lattitudes. The results of those comparisons are explored in this site.
